23 Ladders
A Writing Instrument
09 JUN 2026

23 Ladders is built around a single constraint: you never write words, only choose them.

The Cut-Up Generator follows a method developed by William Burroughs and Brion Gysin in the 1950s — take two texts, break them apart, and let the collision produce something neither contained. Habit is the enemy of language. The technique breaks it.

The Word Ladder is drawn from Jeff Tweedy's songwriting practice: pairs of verbs and nouns, generated until something holds. You are curating, not composing. The words are already there.

Permutation — Your selected words treated as a complete set. Every possible ordering is generated. A sentence has one reading. Ten words have over three million arrangements. Choose the one that surprises you.

4-Quadrant — Both texts cut into four volatile sections and reshuffled across their boundaries. Burroughs called this "cutting in" — forcing contact between passages that would never meet. The meaning that emerges wasn't in either source.

Fold-In — Two texts spliced at their midpoints, or scattered word by word. The original method: fold one printed page onto another and read across the join. What's produced belongs to neither source.

Library — Curated words drawn from literary source texts. Intelligent drift. The pool skews toward precise, evocative language.

Field — Words from your own scans. Higher drift, stranger combinations. The vocabulary becomes yours.

Blend — Library and Field combined. Both pools active.

Chaos — The full pool, no scoring, no weighting. Pure verb-noun collision. Use it when the other modes feel too considered.
